At a Glance:

Business in America Today

With the advent of the Internet and growth of massive multi-national supply chains over the past decade, small businesses and entrepreneurship have become a holistic and integral part of the contemporary American culture and economy. For relatively little start-up cost, anyone with an idea or talent can create, grow, and export their brand to the rest of the world. This map is designed to display a snapshot of how American businesses exist today, providing a variety of indicators for small businesses as well as some contextual data involving corporations to demonstrate the overall makeup of the American economy.

Small Businesses Today

The American Small Business model has allowed for anyone to create and nurture a brand for themselves, and if done successfully, can lead to a very fruitful lifestyle and career. This path however often requires both dedication, expertise in one's craft, and arguably even some luck. According to data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 20% of small businesses fail in their first year of operation, and 50% of them fail after 5 years of operating.

Those who have found success would say the risk is entirely worth it, a plethora of rags-to-riches stories exist in our history. A notable case study within that group however are Americans who identify as a minority, person of color, or reside in a marginalized community. The prospect of creating a small business for themselves can often be a path that leads them out of, or at the very least, mitigate societal inequality and structural oppression they may face. The small business owner may not be powerful enough to continuously lobby congress or vertically integrate every stage of a multi-national supply chain, but they may be powerful enough to end a multi-generational ancestral history of oppression and poverty, which some would say is worth more than an entire monopoly of power.

Corporations Today

The topic of corporations in America is an inherently controversial one, even the data on the map above hints at this. For example, go ahead and try setting the attribute to display the "Percentage of State Businesses that are Small Businesses" and select New York. You will notice that New York stands at the top of the category with 99.8% of its businesses designated as a small business. However, if you change the attribute to "Percentage Employed by Small Businesses", you can see that only 50.2% of New York is employed by them. What this is telling us in context is that a very small handful of mega-corporations manage to employ nearly half of the New York's workforce, despite only making up .2% of total businesses (note that this share is also consists of public-sector jobs which is not included in this study).

To provide a more global context to this American-centered study, take a look at the graphic to the right that displays where the world's top 1000 companies originate from. Unsurprisingly, the United States has the largest share of these companies with a total of 404, nearly half of the entire list and triple that of the next largest shareholder (China).

The balance between small businesses and corporations is an important one to maintain. Corporations are often an integral component of those smaller company's supply chains, often acting as manufacturers or as intermediaries between firm and consumer. Too heavy a hand on either side however can lead to logistical inefficiencies or embedded corruption, which arguably is rampant in our current structure.
